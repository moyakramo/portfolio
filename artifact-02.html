ARTIFACT 02

StudyBridge: Designing, Building, and Testing an AI Study Assistant

Generative AI Prompt Engineering Design Thinking

A working AI chatbot that coaches graduate and online learners through planning, understanding, and studying, without doing their graded work for them. Designed with a human-centered framework, built on Chatbase, and tested against explicit academic-integrity boundaries.

Chat with StudyBridge → View the overview ↓ Full documentation

Introduction

This artifact is the product of a three-stage AI lab. The first stage was hands-on practice with three categories of generative AI tools: a general large language model, a custom GPT, and STORM, a Stanford research assistant. The second stage applied design thinking, a human-centered problem-solving framework. The third stage combined both: planning, building, testing, and iterating a live AI study assistant named StudyBridge, deployed as a shareable chatbot that anyone can try. The full build documentation, including the system prompt and real test transcripts, is embedded below.

Objective

To move from using AI tools to building one: translate a real user need into a working assistant with a defined persona, an ordered interaction flow, and firm ethical boundaries, then verify through testing that every rule holds up under pressure. The target users are working adult learners who struggle less with course material and more with unclear next steps, rubric confusion, and time management.

The Assistant

StudyBridge is live. It greets students, states what it will and will not help with, and asks three intake questions before advising. It breaks assignments into subtasks on a timeline, suggests study techniques like active recall and spaced practice, and explains APA 7 formatting. It refuses to write graded work, invent citations, or answer graded questions, and each of those boundaries was tested directly.

Process

Practiced with three generative AI tools and compared their behavior, including running one structured prompt across three sessions to observe output variability. Applied the design thinking sequence (empathy, define, ideate, prototype, test) to define the user, the problem statement, and an ethical boundary: the assistant must never produce graded work. Answered ten planning questions covering objective, knowledge, boundaries, tone, flow, and success criteria. Reviewed the build platform's privacy statement and terms before deploying. Built the assistant on Chatbase with a custom system prompt and a reference document, then ran one test scenario per rule, tuned the instructions where behavior fell short, and retested until every boundary held.

Tools & Technologies

Chatbase (chatbot build and deployment) · ChatGPT (prompt variability testing) · Universal Primer custom GPT · STORM (Stanford research assistant) · System prompt design · Design thinking framework.

Value Proposition

This artifact demonstrates the full lifecycle of a small AI product: identifying a real user need, defining scope and ethical limits before writing a single instruction, encoding behavior in a system prompt, and proving through adversarial testing that the guardrails work. For businesses considering customer-facing AI assistants, the same discipline applies directly: a chatbot is only as trustworthy as the boundaries it was designed and tested against. The live link lets any reader verify the behavior themselves.

Key Learnings

  • Instructions must be specific. Describing the goal was not enough; the assistant behaved reliably only after the steps were rewritten as an explicit sequence and the boundaries as firm rules.
  • Large language models are probabilistic, not deterministic. Identical prompts produce different outputs, so testing a prompt once proves very little.
  • Guardrails need adversarial tests. Each boundary was confirmed by trying to break it, including asking the assistant to write a graded post and to fabricate a citation.
  • Free tools carry real constraints. Reading the platform's terms surfaced message limits, content caps, and a deletion policy that shaped how the assistant was deployed and maintained.
